MFA rollout checklist

MFA is a control, not a magic spell. The rollout fails when enrollment, recovery, and enforcement are treated as afterthoughts. Use this checklist to deploy MFA with minimal disruption.

1) Define your threat model

  • What are you defending against: phishing, SIM swap, credential stuffing, session theft, insider risk?
  • Which users/actions need higher assurance: finance, admins, production access, payroll, customer data exports?
  • What “phishing resistant” means for you: passkeys/WebAuthn or FIDO2 keys for critical roles.

2) Decide your MFA policy (simple, enforceable)

  • Baseline: MFA required for all users.
  • Step-up: higher assurance for high-risk actions (admin, financial transfers, policy changes).
  • Recovery: documented and testable (not “email the admin”).
  • Exceptions: time-bound, tracked, and reviewed monthly.

If you can’t enforce it automatically, it isn’t policy—it’s advice.

3) Enrollment plan

Enrollment UX

  • Make enrollment the first login experience during a “soft launch” period.
  • Offer 2–3 methods (e.g., authenticator app + passkey/security key + backup codes).
  • Explain why in one paragraph: risk reduction + what changes for the user.

Support readiness

  • Prepare helpdesk scripts for device loss and new phone setup.
  • Define identity proof for resets (manager approval? HRIS match? video?).
  • Set a “break-glass” admin process with audit trails.

4) Recovery and account rescue (the hidden failure mode)

  • Require users to set backup methods at enrollment.
  • Backups must not be the same channel as the primary factor (avoid SMS-only programs).
  • Test recovery: simulate lost device, expired phone, locked authenticator, travel/no signal.

5) Enforcement rollout (phased)

  • Phase 0: visibility only (measure who would fail).
  • Phase 1: enforce for admins + high-risk roles.
  • Phase 2: enforce for all users with grace periods.
  • Phase 3: tighten: block legacy auth, require phishing-resistant for critical actions.

6) Instrumentation

  • Track enrollment completion rate, recovery volume, lockouts, and bypass exceptions.
  • Monitor attack signals (impossible travel, unfamiliar device, repeated push prompts).
  • Review “break-glass” usage monthly.
