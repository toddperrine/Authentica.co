The economics of counterfeits

Counterfeits are often framed as a moral problem. In practice, they are an incentive problem. When the profit is high, the probability of enforcement is low, and buyers are motivated by scarcity or status, fakes flourish.

Why high-end markets attract high-end fakes

High-end categories create a large spread between cost-to-produce and market price. That spread funds sophistication: better materials, better packaging, better stories, better documentation. The object and its supporting evidence are both targeted.

Verification is a tax on the market

In counterfeit-heavy markets, verification becomes a necessary “tax” buyers and sellers pay to transact safely. The more counterfeits, the higher the verification tax. Over time, the market reorganizes around it: escrow, inspections, third-party authentication, and platform guarantees.

Why “trust me” persists

Trust shortcuts are cheaper than verification. Sellers benefit from low friction. Buyers want closure. That gap is where counterfeiters operate: they provide persuasive closure at low cost.

Implication: If a market has scarcity + status + remote transactions, assume verification will be required.

What reduces counterfeit pressure

  • Auditability: methods and records that can be checked independently.
  • Custody transparency: chain-of-custody clarity reduces ambiguity.
  • Aligned incentives: third-party services that benefit from accuracy, not sales.
Practical next step: Treat verification as part of transaction cost, not an optional add-on. Start with Trusted Authentication Services.
