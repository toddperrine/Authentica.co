What actually proves authenticity (and what doesn’t)

Evergreen Framework Risk-aware

“Authentic” is often treated as a vibe: the thing looks right, the story feels plausible, the seller seems confident. That is not proof. In high-stakes categories—luxury, art, collectibles, wine, watches—proof is a structure. It is a set of signals that agree with each other, survive stress, and can be audited by someone other than the seller.

Working definition: Authenticity is the claim. Verification is the method. Provenance is the evidence trail that makes the claim durable.

1) Provenance is the highest-order signal

Provenance is not a certificate. It’s an evidence trail: receipts, auction records, service records, gallery history, shipping documents, photographs, and credible witnesses. It answers a simple question: Where has this object been, and who can independently corroborate that?

Provenance is powerful because it is difficult to fabricate consistently. Single documents can be forged; a coherent trail across time is harder.

2) Chain of custody matters more than a story

A story is a narrative. Chain of custody is a sequence of custody events: who held the item, when, and under what conditions. For some categories (wine, collectibles, archival items), custody history can be the difference between “likely” and “bankable.”

3) Independent inspection beats self-attestation

Inspection should be performed by a party who does not benefit from the result. “Authenticated by the seller” is not authentication. Independent inspection combines physical checks (materials, construction, wear patterns) with references (known exemplars, brand archives, production-era details).

4) Reference archives are how experts avoid being persuaded

The most convincing fakes are convincing because they target human psychology. The antidote is reference: catalogs, archives, internal brand documentation, and known exemplars. When experts say “it matches,” they mean it matches a reference set.

5) The strongest proof is convergence

In practice, you rarely get a single perfect signal. What you want is convergence: multiple independent signals that point to the same conclusion. Provenance aligns with inspection. Serial details align with production era. Service records align with observed wear. The item’s story aligns with the market’s record.

What doesn’t prove authenticity

  • A single certificate (especially if it can’t be verified independently).
  • A seller’s confidence or “guarantee” without method.
  • A screenshot of a database entry without auditability.
  • “It came from a reputable place” as a substitute for evidence.
  • AI-generated analysis without transparent inputs and limitations.
