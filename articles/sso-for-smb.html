SSO for SMB: when it’s worth it

SSO is not “security by default.” It’s an operational control: centralized access, fewer passwords, faster offboarding, and consistent MFA/policy enforcement.

SSO is worth it when

  • You have >10–15 critical apps and onboarding/offboarding is messy.
  • People reuse passwords across apps (credential stuffing risk is real).
  • You need consistent MFA enforcement across services.
  • You have compliance pressure (SOC2-ish expectations, customer security reviews).
  • You want lifecycle automation (SCIM) so access follows job changes.

SSO is not worth it (yet) when

  • You have a small number of apps and a single admin can manage access cleanly.
  • Your org can’t commit to basic identity hygiene (joiner/mover/leaver discipline).
  • Recovery and helpdesk processes don’t exist (you’ll amplify lockouts).

Common failure modes

“SSO for some apps” drift

SSO adoption becomes partial, leaving password islands. Attackers use the weakest app as the entry point.

Offboarding gaps

If HRIS isn’t driving deprovisioning, SSO won’t save you. Termination must disable access everywhere.

Poor recovery design

Centralized login means centralized failure. If recovery is weak, you create support churn and unsafe bypasses.

Overpaying for features you won’t use

Know your must-haves: SAML/OIDC breadth, SCIM, conditional access, admin workflows, reporting.

What “good” looks like

  • All critical apps federated (SAML/OIDC) with MFA enforced.
  • SCIM enabled for key apps (automatic provisioning/deprovisioning).
  • Admins use higher assurance (passkeys/security keys) with break-glass audited.
  • Conditional access policies exist (device posture, geo/risk, step-up on sensitive actions).
