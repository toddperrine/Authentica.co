Passkeys: a pragmatic guide

Passkeys can meaningfully reduce phishing and credential replay, but only if you treat recovery, device loss, and admin flows as first-class design constraints.

Updated: 2025-12-26

Summary

  • Passkeys reduce phishing by eliminating shared secrets (passwords) in normal login flows.
  • Recovery is the real attack surface: if fallback is “email reset + SMS,” your security story collapses.
  • Roll out in sequence: admins and high-risk actions first; broad end-user adoption later.
  • Plan for device churn: multiple devices, lost phones, and BYOD are the default, not edge cases.

What passkeys are (in operational terms)

A passkey is a phishing-resistant credential based on public-key cryptography (FIDO2/WebAuthn). The “secret” never leaves the device or platform authenticator. The relying party (your app/service) stores a public key and verifies signed challenges.

In practice: users authenticate with a platform prompt (biometric/PIN), not by typing a password. That materially changes attacker economics for credential stuffing and social engineering.

When passkeys help the most

  • Phishing-heavy environments (contractors, distributed teams, high email volume).
  • High-value admin consoles and privileged actions (billing, user mgmt, API keys).
  • Consumer apps where password reuse is rampant and account takeover is expensive.
  • Organizations migrating off SMS-based MFA without crushing user experience.

Failure modes to watch

1) “Secure login, insecure recovery”

The most common failure is implementing passkeys but leaving recovery as a weak link: email reset, SMS OTP, or helpdesk “verify a few facts.” Attackers will route around your strong auth.

2) Single-device assumptions

Users switch phones, wipe laptops, lose devices, and enroll new devices constantly. If your model assumes “one phone forever,” you will end up adding insecure fallbacks later under pressure.

3) Mixed policy environments

Some users will have passkeys; others won’t. If your policy and UX don’t handle mixed states cleanly, you’ll create support debt and inconsistent security outcomes.

4) Admin bypass and break-glass gaps

Admins are high-risk. If admins can bypass passkeys via a legacy path, attackers will target that path. You also need an explicit break-glass approach that doesn’t silently become “permanent bypass.”

Implementation sequence (recommended)

  1. Define recovery first. Decide what “lost device” means and what the user must present to re-establish trust. Prefer strong step-up options over “email link + SMS.”
  2. Secure privileged actions. Require phishing-resistant auth for admin login and any action that changes identity, billing, or security posture.
  3. Introduce passkeys as an option for end-users, with clear enrollment UX and support docs.
  4. Measure adoption and support burden. Track: enrollments, fallback usage, reset requests, and lockouts.
  5. Move to policy. Once you trust your recovery model and adoption is healthy, require passkeys (or require them for high-risk cohorts and flows).

Decision questions (use these with vendors or internal teams)

  • What are the supported recovery paths, and how are they secured against social engineering?
  • How does enrollment work across multiple devices and platforms (iOS/Android/Windows/macOS)?
  • Can we require passkeys for specific actions (step-up) without requiring them for all logins?
  • What happens during outages? Is there a safe “degraded mode”?
  • How do logs show fallback usage vs passkey usage (so we can detect policy bypass)?
