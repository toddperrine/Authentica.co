Passkeys: a pragmatic guide

Passkeys reduce phishing risk and password reuse. They also introduce operational questions: enrollment, device changes, shared devices, and account recovery. This guide focuses on what works in real deployments.

What a passkey is (plain language)

A passkey is a phishing-resistant credential based on public-key cryptography (WebAuthn/FIDO2). The private key stays on the user’s device (or secure enclave); the server stores a public key. Logging in proves possession without sending a reusable password.

What passkeys solve

  • Phishing of passwords (the credential isn’t typed or reusable).
  • Credential stuffing (there’s no shared password to reuse).
  • Weak password policies (passkeys bypass the problem entirely).

What passkeys do not solve

  • Session theft/token hijacking after login (you still need session controls).
  • Compromised endpoints (malware can abuse sessions and approved actions).
  • Bad authorization (passkeys authenticate identity, not least-privilege access).

Deployment patterns that work

Workforce high assurance

Admins and finance roles: require passkeys/security keys for privileged actions; keep backup factors for recovery.

Customer “step-up”

Allow password login, but require passkey for sensitive actions (payout changes, exports, account takeover signals).

Passwordless-first (careful)

Works best when you have strong account recovery, multi-device enrollment, and clear “lost device” flows.

Hybrid onboarding

Offer passkeys during first login, but don’t hard-enforce until you’ve achieved high enrollment coverage.

Recovery is the real design problem

  • Require multi-device enrollment (phone + laptop where possible).
  • Provide backup methods (codes, alternative verified device, helpdesk verification).
  • Define identity proof for resets. “Email the admin” is not a strategy.
